Christopher Bosley

Meet Christopher

Christopher Bosley spent over a decade building an impressive career in tech, rising to executive roles at companies like Epic Games where he worked on Rocket League—developing matchmaking systems, building anti-cheat frameworks, and pioneering methods to quantify player performance. He directed AI and data organizations, managed multi-million dollar initiatives, and mentored countless professionals along the way.

But something was missing.

After completing his Master of Legal Studies at the University of Utah, Christopher realized he'd never fallen out of love with learning—and he wanted to be on the other side of the classroom. His dream? Teaching game analytics at his alma mater's newly launched Division of Games, helping the next generation of professionals bridge the gap between industry and academia.

The credentials were there. The passion was undeniable. But the path forward was anything but clear.

The Problem

Christopher approached academic job applications the same way he'd approached everything in his career: dive in and figure it out. He applied to three institutions through online portals and heard almost nothing back. At Utah Valley University, he made it through initial screening rounds but never landed an interview.

The problem wasn't his qualifications—it was how he was presenting them.

His resume read like what it was: a polished executive CV designed to pass ATS filters and impress corporate recruiters. His cover letter led with strategic capabilities and organizational achievements. His teaching statement was, in his own words, "absolute gibberish"—full of philosophy but lacking the concrete evidence hiring committees actually evaluate.

Christopher had taught statistics as an adjunct a decade earlier. He'd published research. He'd built an entire Python curriculum for his analytics team at Epic Games. He'd mentored interns who went on to run departments. But none of this showed up where it mattered—above the fold, in the language academics actually speak.

As he put it: "I have the right content and not the right language. I'm not speaking to people in the language they need to hear."

How Professor Town Helped

In a single strategy session, we pulled back the curtain on how faculty hiring actually works.

Christopher got to see real screening rubrics from universities like Berkeley, Maryland, and Delaware—the actual scoring criteria committees use to evaluate candidates. The revelation was immediate: academic hiring committees don't care about "enterprise systems architecture" or "delivery frameworks." They're looking for evidence of scholarly impact, teaching and mentoring potential, and the ability to attract funding.

Christopher had all of this. He just needed to surface it differently.

We restructured his approach from the ground up:

  • Resume transformation: We moved publications and patents above the fold. Reframed his intern mentorship program as evidence of teaching potential. Translated "secured $15 million in program funding" into language that signals grant-writing capability. Cut the executive summary that everyone skips anyway.
  • Cover letter reframe: Instead of leading with credentials, we flipped the narrative. The new opening: "I'm excited to bring my 15 years of executive experience in the gaming industry to the School of Gaming." Student impact first, qualifications second.
  • Teaching statement overhaul: We shifted from philosophy to evidence. That Python curriculum he built at Epic? That's pedagogical development. The interns who became data scientists? That's measurable student outcomes. The training pipeline he created? That's outcome-based instruction in action.
  • Networking strategy: We identified that adjunct positions rarely go through formal channels—they happen through relationships. Christopher needed to connect with faculty directly, offer to guest lecture, and position himself as the solution to a program that was clearly understaffed.

The Results

Within weeks of implementing these changes, Christopher's trajectory transformed completely.

  • Landed an adjunct mathematics position at Salt Lake Community College. His teaching demo and interview went smoothly—he successfully reframed his industry mentorship experience using academic language, exactly as we'd practiced.
  • Secured a meeting with the Assistant Chair for Academic Affairs at the University of Utah's Division of Games. His warm outreach approach—framing his experience as a value-add and expressing willingness to contribute in multiple ways—got him in the door.
  • Booked to guest lecture and hold workshops in the Games program's producer curriculum. The department is actively discussing adjunct and career-line roles with him once their tenure-track hire is in place for Fall 2026.

In Christopher's words:

"Your method of approaching this warm introduction was great. Thank you for turning this 'I think I'd like to try being a professor' into a reality that's flying like a rocket."
"I really felt like Professor Town was an authoritative research resource. There was obvious evidentiary materials, experience in the field, and an understanding of how the university hiring process works."
"I will absolutely send anyone I know that could use your help to Professor Town. I'll also be, on my own, evangelizing your services because they just work."

Christopher's journey proves what's possible when you stop thinking like an executive and start speaking the language of academia. The credentials were always there. He just needed someone who sits on hiring committees to show him how we actually read applications.
