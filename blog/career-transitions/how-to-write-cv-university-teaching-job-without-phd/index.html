How to Write a CV for a University Teaching Job Without a PhD

By Haaris Mian July 1, 2026 11 min read

Your industry resume will get you filtered out in the first pass. Not because your experience is wrong, but because the document is structured for the wrong audience.

Hiring committees at colleges and universities are scanning for specific signals in a specific sequence. If those signals aren't where screeners expect to find them, your file goes in the wrong pile before anyone reads the substance. I've sat on those committees. Here's what's actually happening on the other side of the table.

How academic hiring committees review applications.

The Market Reality Before We Talk About the CV

Adjunct and contingent faculty now make up approximately 73% of the instructional workforce at U.S. colleges and universities, according to the American Association of University Professors. The number of part-time faculty in the U.S. grew from roughly 200,000 in 1970 to over 700,000 by the early 2020s.

That sounds like opportunity. And it is, but the market has tightened.

In Canada, the federal government capped international study permits in January 2024, limiting intake to approximately 364,000 permits, a 35% reduction from 2023 levels. Canadian post-secondary institutions warned this would cost the sector billions in lost tuition revenue, with some universities projecting cuts to hundreds of staff and faculty positions. Colleges that had been expanding adjunct rosters to handle enrolment growth froze those postings almost immediately.

The same budget pressure dynamic is playing out at U.S. institutions in different forms. Fewer part-time lines are being posted. More duties are being consolidated into single full-time or full-load practitioner positions. That means a role that once attracted 80 applications now attracts 200 or more. One Ontario college dissolved several adjunct contract postings in early 2024 and replaced them with a single "industry practitioner" faculty line. One position, same teaching load, far more competition.

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects postsecondary teacher employment to grow 8% from 2022 to 2032, faster than the average for all occupations. The opportunity is real. But when a single position draws 150 to 400 applications at a U.S. research university, a CV that doesn't clear the first-pass filter doesn't get a second look.

This is the problem we're solving.

The First-Pass Scan: What Committees Actually Look at in the First 90 Seconds

Most advice on academic CV structure is vague. "Put your teaching experience first" doesn't tell you what's actually happening when a committee member opens your file.

Here's the real sequence.

What the Screener Is Hunting For

The initial review, often done by a single committee member or a graduate assistant before the full committee sees anything, is not a read. It's a pattern-match. The screener is looking for four things, roughly in this order:

  1. Terminal degree or approved equivalent. Their eyes go to your education section or the top credential line immediately. If the degree isn't visible in the first visual zone (top third of page one), they have to hunt for it. Hunting creates friction. Friction creates doubt.
  2. Evidence of classroom contact. The phrases they're scanning for: "courses taught," "course name," "curriculum development," "instructor of record," "taught," "facilitated." These words trigger a "yes" reflex. Their absence triggers "unclear fit."
  3. Section headers that match academic norms. Headers like "Teaching Experience," "Courses Taught," "Publications," "Service," and "Professional Development" signal that you understand the format. Headers like "Core Competencies" and "Professional Summary" signal that you don't.
  4. Institutional legibility. Can they quickly understand what you've done in contexts they recognize? A title like "Director of Learning and Development, 3M" registers. A title like "Principal Solutions Architect" may not, without translation.

The screener is not reading your bullets at this stage. They are reading your structure. If the structure doesn't surface those four signals in the first scan zone, your file is moved before anyone gets to your substance.

Committee members in actual deliberation rooms say things like "I can't tell if this person has ever stood in front of a class" and "this reads like a LinkedIn profile, not an academic CV." Those are the real rejection reasons. They're rarely communicated to applicants.

The Specific Zones That Determine First-Pass Outcome

Top third of page one. This is where the credential, the teaching signal, and the section header hierarchy land. If your industry job titles dominate this space without academic framing, you've already lost the scan.

Section headers across the full document. Screeners run their eyes down the left margin to read headers before they read content. The header sequence is your CV's table of contents. "Teaching Experience" in position two or three tells the screener exactly where to go next.

First bullet under each section. When a screener does slow down to read, they read the first bullet under each header and skim or skip the rest. Your strongest, most teaching-relevant item needs to be first, not saved for last.

This is the physical reality of first-pass screening. Structure your document around it.

The Structural Difference Between an Industry Resume and an Academic CV

These are not the same document with different content. They are different genres with different logic.

The Industry Resume Logic

Concise. One to two pages. Achievement-oriented bullets with metrics. A summary statement at the top. Reverse chronological work history leading the structure. Designed to be read quickly by a recruiter evaluating transferable competencies.

The Academic CV Logic

Comprehensive. As long as it needs to be. Organized by category, not chronology. No summary statement. Sections like "Teaching Experience," "Publications," "Conference Presentations," "Grants and Fellowships," "Service," and "Professional Memberships" form the backbone. Designed to give a committee a complete scholarly and pedagogical record.

For industry professionals transitioning to teaching, the goal is not to shorten your document. It's to restructure it.

Here's the section order that works for a practitioner applicant without a formal classroom record:

  1. Education (lead with this, always, because the credential question gets answered immediately)
  2. Teaching and Training Experience (reframed industry teaching content, more on this below)
  3. Professional Experience (your industry titles and roles, after the teaching section)
  4. Publications, Cases, and Curriculum Materials (anything you've written, contributed to, or developed)
  5. Conference Presentations and Speaking (every panel, keynote, workshop you've led)
  6. Professional Affiliations and Certifications
  7. Service (mentorship programs, advisory boards, industry association leadership)

This structure tells the screener: credential, teaching credibility, then industry depth. In that order.

An industry resume built the opposite way, with two paragraphs of professional summary and a chronological work history, puts the committee in the position of digging for what they need. Most won't dig. They'll move on.

Consider the civil engineer whose materials I reviewed. She had 20 years of project leadership, a master's degree, and genuine teaching experience buried on page three under "Additional Activities." Guest lectures. Technical training delivery. Mentorship of junior engineers. All of it invisible in the scan zone. She was filtered out before anyone reached that section. Her qualifications were sufficient. Her structure failed her.

How to Surface Teaching Credibility Without Formal Classroom Experience

This is where most industry professionals get stuck. They assume they have nothing to put in a "Teaching Experience" section because they've never taught a credited course. That assumption is wrong.

What counts, translated correctly:

Corporate training programs you designed or delivered. List these with the same specificity you'd use for a course. Name the training, identify the audience, note the duration and enrollment where possible. "Designed and delivered a 16-hour financial modeling workshop for 45 mid-level analysts, Q1 2022" is a teaching line.

Executive education or professional development workshops. If you've presented at an industry workshop, led a half-day seminar, or designed a professional certification module, that goes under teaching, not under professional experience.

Guest lectures at colleges or universities. List the institution, the course name, the professor who invited you, and the date. Even a single guest lecture, listed properly, tells the committee you have classroom exposure.

Mentorship and coaching programs. Formal mentorship roles, especially those with structured learning goals or assessments, belong in the teaching section. Informal mentorship does not.

Conference workshops and technical sessions. Presenting to peers in a professional context demonstrates pedagogical confidence. List these under "Conference Presentations" with the session format noted (workshop, panel, keynote).

The framing matters. Don't write "Presented at industry conference." Write "Led 90-minute workshop on supply chain risk modeling for 60 practitioners, APICS Annual Conference, 2023."

The CFO who restructured her CV for a Professor of Practice role is a useful example. She led with a "Teaching and Curriculum" section listing executive education workshops she had designed, MBA guest lectures with course names and enrollment numbers, and a business school case study she co-authored. Her industry titles followed. She received an interview. The committee's feedback was that they "could immediately see she understood the classroom environment."

That's the outcome of correct framing.

The Terminal Degree Question: What Substitutes for a PhD and Where

You do not need a PhD to get a university or college teaching position. The credential threshold varies significantly by institution type and discipline, and in many fields a PhD is neither expected nor particularly valued.

Where a Master's Degree Is Typically Sufficient

Community colleges. Community colleges employ the largest share of part-time and adjunct faculty and are frequently the entry point for industry professionals transitioning to teaching, according to the American Association of Community Colleges. A master's degree is the standard minimum for most credit-bearing courses at this level.

Professional programs in business. MBA programs, undergraduate business programs, and executive education units at many universities actively recruit practitioners. An MBA, CPA, or CFA paired with substantial industry experience is competitive. An MBA with no industry depth is not.

Engineering and construction management programs. A licensed Professional Engineer (PE) with project experience can be highly competitive for lecturer and adjunct roles in civil, mechanical, and construction management departments. The PE designation is understood by committee members in those disciplines.

Law, nursing, and social work programs. A JD, MD, or MSW is the expected terminal credential in those professional programs. A PhD in those fields is unusual and sometimes viewed as a mismatch for practice-oriented roles.

Professor of Practice tracks. Many research universities now offer formal Professor of Practice positions explicitly designed for industry professionals. These roles often have no PhD requirement and evaluate candidates primarily on practitioner depth and teaching potential.

How Committees Decide Whether a Non-PhD Candidate Is Safe to Advance

This is the informal layer that never appears in hiring guidelines.

Committees at teaching-focused institutions use a set of heuristics when evaluating non-traditional credentials. They're looking for signals that make the candidate feel credible to a skeptical search committee chair or dean.

Those signals include:

  • A recognized professional credential (CPA, PE, JD, MD, PMP in some contexts) that the committee can point to as evidence of equivalent rigor
  • An affiliation with a known industry partner or employer
  • Any formal connection to academic environments, even as a guest or industry advisor
  • Evidence that the candidate has engaged with academic literature in the field, shown through references, case co-authorship, or the language used in the cover letter

None of these appear on a published scoring rubric. All of them influence which files get advanced.

The practical implication: if you don't have a PhD, your CV needs to do extra work to make the committee feel confident advancing your file. That means leaning harder into the professional credential line, being explicit about any academic-adjacent activities, and structuring the document to communicate institutional literacy through the format itself.

Four Myths That Will Get Your Application Filtered Out

Myth: Committees read CVs cover-to-cover before making any judgment.

They don't. Initial screening involves a rapid visual scan for specific signals. A CV that buries those signals in the wrong location fails the first filter regardless of overall strength.

Myth: An academic CV should be concise, one to two pages, with a strong summary at the top.

This is the industry resume format. It is the wrong format for an academic application. Academic CVs are intentionally comprehensive. For industry professionals, the goal is not to shorten but to restructure. Surface teaching-relevant content into the first visible sections while keeping the complete professional record intact.

Myth: Without a PhD, you cannot get a university teaching position.

Many institutions, particularly community colleges, professional programs, and Professor of Practice tracks at research universities, actively hire candidates whose terminal credential is a master's degree or professional qualification. The credential threshold varies sharply by institution type and discipline.

Myth: Industry experience speaks for itself.

It doesn't. Titles, metrics, and company names that carry weight in corporate contexts are often opaque to a committee evaluating teaching potential. A title like "Principal Solutions Architect" or "VP of Revenue Operations" tells a humanities professor on a hiring committee very little about whether you can run a seminar. Industry experience must be explicitly reframed in terms of pedagogical relevance.

What Has Changed in the Current Hiring Cycle

The bar for what a first-pass CV must communicate has moved.

When adjunct lines were plentiful and competition was moderate, a committee could afford to chase down promising candidates whose files were ambiguous. A phone call could clarify whether that "training delivery" bullet meant standing in front of 200 people or sending an email with a PDF attachment.

Fewer positions and more applicants per position has eliminated that tolerance. When a role draws 200-plus applications, the screener's job is to reduce the pile to 20, not to advocate for unclear cases. The candidates who make the long list are the ones whose CVs do not require interpretation.

The constrained hiring environment, especially visible in Canada following the 2024 international student permit cap but present across North American institutions managing budget pressure, means the cost of a structurally flawed CV is higher than it was five years ago. The same experience, packaged in the wrong format, now results in a faster rejection.

The Pay Reality

Before you optimize your application materials, know what you're applying for.

The median annual wage for postsecondary teachers in the U.S. was $84,380 in May 2023, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics. That figure covers full-time positions across institution types.

For adjunct and per-course roles, the picture is different. The Coalition on the Academic Workforce has reported median pay for adjunct faculty at roughly $3,500 per course section, with wide variation by institution type.

For industry professionals with strong credentials and salary histories, the per-course adjunct rate is often not the financial draw. The draw is usually a transition path, a meaningful side engagement, or positioning for a full-time practitioner faculty role. Know which you're building toward, because the CV strategy differs slightly depending on your end goal.

Where to Go From Here

If you've read this far, you probably have enough experience to qualify for a teaching role at the right institution. The obstacle is usually not credentials. It's translation.

Translating an industry career into a document that reads correctly to an academic committee is a specific skill, and most industry professionals underestimate how different the genre conventions are until their files come back without an interview.
